Advanced Modeling: Gradient Boosting and Event Study – Milestone 3


Overview

This entry documents two major additions in Milestone 3: the event-study analysis and the
introduction of Gradient Boosting as a third machine learning model. Together these additions
address the two main gaps from Milestone 2: understanding the dynamic shape of sanction effects
over time, and determining whether a more powerful model can improve predictive accuracy.


Event Study Analysis

The event study is a quasi-experimental design that examines how child mortality evolves
in the years before and after a country's first sanction episode. I defined year 0 as
the first year a country had sanction_active = 1, and tracked child mortality from 5 years
before to 5 years after.

The key insight from the event study is that child mortality in eventually-sanctioned countries
was already elevated before sanctions were imposed. This confirms what the fixed-effects
regression suggested: selection bias is real. Countries that get sanctioned are not comparable
to countries that never face sanctions, even before the sanctions begin.

The event study also reveals that child mortality trends do not show a sharp break at year 0 in
many countries. Instead, the trajectory appears to be a gradual trend that predates the sanction
episode. This is important for interpreting the fixed-effects estimates and for understanding the
limits of the causal claims the project can make.

I normalized the event study to year −1 (the year immediately before sanctions) to focus on
changes relative to the pre-sanction baseline rather than levels. This makes it easier to see
whether there is any departure from the pre-sanction trajectory after sanctions are imposed.


Gradient Boosting: Why it was chosen

Milestone 2 used Linear Regression as a baseline and Random Forest as an advanced model.
For Milestone 3 I added Gradient Boosting (sklearn's GradientBoostingRegressor) for three reasons:

1. Gradient Boosting typically outperforms Random Forest on tabular structured data because it
   builds trees sequentially, with each tree correcting the residuals of the previous one.
   Random Forest builds trees in parallel without this error correction mechanism.

2. The learning rate and subsample parameters provide explicit regularization, which reduces
   the tendency to overfit on the training period (1995–2010) and fail to generalize to
   the test period (2015–2024).

3. Adding a third model completes the comparison arc: from simple linear (interpretable,
   underfits nonlinearities) to tree ensemble (flexible but potentially overfit) to gradient
   boosted trees (balanced regularization).


Hyperparameter Choices

I used n_estimators=300, max_depth=4, learning_rate=0.05, subsample=0.8, min_samples_leaf=10.

- max_depth=4 prevents individual trees from becoming too complex and memorizing training data
- learning_rate=0.05 (slow) with more trees is a well-established regularization strategy
- subsample=0.8 introduces stochastic variation similar to bagging, which reduces variance
- min_samples_leaf=10 prevents trees from splitting on very small node groups

These are standard settings from the literature for tabular data. I did not run a full
grid search given the panel structure of the data and the risk of optimizing for a
particular time split at the expense of generalizability.


Model Comparison

The three-model comparison shows that Gradient Boosting achieves the best validation R² and
is comparable to Random Forest on the test set. The relatively modest improvement on the test
set (compared to Random Forest) confirms that there is a natural ceiling on predictive accuracy
for this problem: child mortality from 2015+ depends on global trends and country-specific
shocks that are not well represented in the 1995–2010 training period.

The three models collectively demonstrate the full predictive modeling stage: a simple
interpretable baseline, a nonlinear ensemble baseline, and an optimized gradient-based model.
