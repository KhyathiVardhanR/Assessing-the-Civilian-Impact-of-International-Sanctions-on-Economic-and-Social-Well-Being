Fixed-Effects Panel Regression – Milestone 3


Overview

This stage introduced the most methodologically rigorous analysis of the project: fixed-effects (FE)
panel regression. This step directly addresses the key limitation of Milestones 1 and 2, which used
predictive models that could not isolate the causal effect of sanctions from selection bias.

The central problem is that sanctioned countries are not randomly selected. Countries that face
sanctions tend to be in political or economic crisis already, which means their civilian welfare
outcomes may be deteriorating for reasons unrelated to the sanctions themselves. A naive OLS
regression comparing sanctioned to non-sanctioned countries conflates the sanction effect with
all the pre-existing differences between these groups.


What Fixed Effects Does

The within-estimator (used here through the demeaning approach) removes all time-invariant
country-level differences from the estimation. By subtracting each country's own time-average
from every variable, the model uses only within-country variation over time. The sanction
coefficient then reflects: when a country transitions from no sanctions to sanctions, what
happens to child mortality on average, holding year effects and economic controls constant?

Year fixed effects (implemented as dummy variables for each year) absorb global trends, such as
worldwide improvements in child health due to advances in medicine, that would otherwise bias
the estimates.


Variable Selection

The dependent variable remains child_mortality_u5. This was the correct choice because it is the
most complete outcome variable and most directly sensitive to the humanitarian channels that
sanctions target — medicine, food, energy.

The treatment variables are sanction_active (binary) and sanction_intensity_index (continuous).
Using both captures whether any sanction is present and how severe it is, allowing the model to
detect dose-response effects.

Control variables include gdp_growth, inflation_rate, conflict_incidence, conflict_intensity,
unemployment_rate, log_total_trade_exposure, and regime_score. These were chosen because they
are established confounders in the literature: armed conflict can cause welfare deterioration
that looks like a sanction effect if not controlled; regime type correlates with both sanction
imposition and welfare provision.


Key Finding

After controlling for country and year fixed effects, the within-country sanction effect on child
mortality is substantially smaller than the unadjusted descriptive gap. This is an honest and
important finding: it does not mean sanctions have no effect, but it does mean that much of the
raw statistical association between sanctions and poor welfare reflects selection bias. Countries
that get sanctioned were already different.

This result has important policy implications: policies targeting sanctioned countries for
humanitarian relief need to account for the fact that pre-existing vulnerability is a major
driver of poor welfare outcomes, not just the sanctions themselves.


Decisions Made

I chose HC1 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors rather than classical OLS standard errors.
In a panel dataset with countries of very different sizes and income levels, residual variance is
unlikely to be constant across observations. Robust standard errors are more conservative and
more defensible in this setting.

I implemented the within-transformation manually using pandas rather than relying on a
specialized panel econometrics package. This makes the code more transparent and reproducible
without requiring installation of additional dependencies beyond what was already in the project.
The mathematics are identical to what a package like linearmodels would produce.
