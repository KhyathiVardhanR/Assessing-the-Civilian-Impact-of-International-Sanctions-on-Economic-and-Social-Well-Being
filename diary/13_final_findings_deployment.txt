Final Findings, Deployment, and Project Completion – Milestone 3


Overview

This final entry documents the completed project and reflects on the full data science cycle
from problem formulation to deployed interactive tool. It also explains the key substantive
findings and the decisions made in communicating them.


Final Architecture of the Project

The completed project integrates four data sources (sanctions, trade, welfare, political)
into a country-year panel dataset covering approximately 190 countries from 1995 to 2024.
The full data science cycle proceeds through:

1. Problem formulation and data acquisition (Milestone 1)
2. Relational database design and storage (Milestone 1)
3. Exploratory data analysis (Milestone 1)
4. Data cleaning and standardization (Milestone 2)
5. Feature engineering — lags, interactions, gender gaps, log transforms (Milestone 2)
6. Baseline and advanced predictive modeling (Milestone 2)
7. Static dashboard for communication (Milestone 2)
8. Fixed-effects causal regression (Milestone 3)
9. Event-study design (Milestone 3)
10. Gradient Boosting as a third model (Milestone 3)
11. Heterogeneous effects by sanction type and political regime (Milestone 3)
12. Multi-outcome analysis (child mortality, school enrollment, unemployment) (Milestone 3)
13. Streamlit interactive deployment (Milestone 3)


Deployed Tool

The project is deployed as a Streamlit dashboard (app.py) with six pages:
- Overview: research framework and methodology summary
- Data Explorer: filter and explore the panel dataset interactively
- Sanction Impact: descriptive comparisons across outcomes and over time
- Model Results: all three models, feature importance, prediction quality
- Advanced Analysis: fixed-effects regression, event study, multi-outcome FE
- Findings: key conclusions, variable justification, limitations

The dashboard loads all outputs from the processed data folder and presents them
through Plotly interactive charts. It is designed to communicate the project findings
to a non-technical audience while preserving methodological depth for reviewers.


Key Substantive Findings

1. Unadjusted descriptive gap exists but is misleading: in raw comparisons, sanctioned
   countries appear to have worse outcomes across all five dimensions. However, this reflects
   a mix of sanction effects and the fact that sanctioned countries were already different
   before sanctions were imposed.

2. Fixed-effects estimation across all five outcomes tells a consistent story: after
   controlling for country and year fixed effects, the within-country sanction coefficient
   is small and NOT statistically significant for any of the five outcomes (all p > 0.05).
   Specifically:
   - Child mortality (Health): coef=+0.64, p=0.69
   - School enrollment (Education): coef=+0.05, p=0.97
   - Girls enrollment (Gender equity): coef=+0.52, p=0.67
   - Unemployment (Economic): coef=-0.16, p=0.68
   - Inflation (Price stability): coef=+3.37, p=0.24

3. Composite Civilian Well-Being Index: After standardizing and combining all five
   dimensions, the gap between sanctioned (94.8) and non-sanctioned (94.2) countries
   disappears entirely. The within-country effect of sanctions on composite well-being
   is near zero after FE.

4. The event study confirms this: child mortality in sanctioned countries was declining
   over the full event window (-5 to +5), tracking the global downward trend. There is
   no visible break at the sanction onset year.

5. The best predictive model (Gradient Boosting) achieves Validation R²=0.83 and
   Test R²=0.65. The most important predictors are female primary school enrollment,
   poverty rate, and school enrollment — structural welfare indicators that pre-date
   and dominate over sanction variables.

6. Authoritarian regimes show larger descriptive welfare gaps under sanctions in
   unadjusted comparisons, but after FE the regime × sanction interaction weakens,
   again suggesting selection bias is the primary driver.

The core finding of this project is methodologically rigorous and honest: the widely-cited
descriptive evidence that sanctions harm civilians is substantially driven by the fact that
the countries targeted by sanctions were already poorer and more vulnerable before sanctions
were imposed. Careful panel identification using fixed effects reduces the estimated sanction
effect to near zero across all five civilian well-being dimensions studied.


Variable Selection: Final Justification

The decision to use child_mortality_u5 as the primary dependent variable was correct.
It is the most complete, most internationally comparable, and most policy-relevant outcome
available in the dataset. Poverty rate and Gini index are theoretically better welfare
measures but are too sparse (68% and 74% missing) to support robust panel analysis.

Using both sanction_active and sanction_intensity_index as treatment variables captures
both the presence and severity of economic coercion — an important distinction given
that targeted visa bans and comprehensive trade embargoes are categorically different.


What I Would Do Differently

If additional time were available, I would pursue three improvements:

1. Instrumental variables estimation: Finding a valid instrument for sanction imposition
   (a variable that predicts which countries get sanctioned but does not directly affect
   welfare) would allow stronger causal claims than fixed effects alone.

2. Synthetic control method: For major sanction episodes (Iran, Russia, North Korea),
   constructing a synthetic comparison country from the weighted combination of
   non-sanctioned countries would provide sharper case-level causal estimates.

3. Imputation of poverty data: Using multiple imputation to recover some of the missing
   poverty rate and Gini index values would allow welfare channel analysis through the
   inequality dimension that is currently limited by data sparseness.


Reflection on the Full Data Science Cycle

This project demonstrates that a rigorous data science analysis requires far more than
fitting a model. The largest methodological contribution of Milestone 3 — fixed-effects
regression — is not about improving R² but about asking whether the model is actually
answering the right question. A model that explains 99% of variance in child mortality
(as the initial leakage-prone model did) can be completely misleading if it is measuring
the wrong thing.

The honest finding — that sanctions are associated with worse welfare but that selection
bias accounts for a significant portion of the gap — is more valuable than a model
that produces large, superficially impressive sanction coefficients. Real data science
means following the evidence wherever it leads, even when the answer is more complicated
than expected.
